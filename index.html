A timeline of the Gulf countries' oil revolution

Spans nearly 100 years and provides insight on several countries in the MENA Region. Contains many images and resources for further reading. Made by Simon Bullotta. Enjoy!

Pre-1932

The search for oil began due to a few mounting factors: The Anglo-Persian Oil Company had previously discovered oil in Persia, the demand for oil rose greatly due to WWI, and the Great Depression hurt west Saudi Arabia greatly, requiring them to look for a different source of income.

1932

Bahrain Petroleum Company discovers oil in Bahrain. They build a oil well, and it produces roughly 400 oil barrels per hour, shifting the local economy to focus on oil well construction and refining. This moment sparks motivation to search for oil across the rest of the Arabian peninsula.

1938

On March 3, 1938, the California Arabian Standard Oil Company ("CASOC") finally uncovered oil in Saudi Arabia. Also in this year, oil was discovered in Kuwait at Burgan field (Abd el-aal et al. 117–144).

1939-1945

The upheaval caused by WWII slowed down oil production in Saudi Arabia significantly. Some of the factors include foreign experts leaving to go home, disrupted shipping routes like the Mediterranean sea, and, in 1940, "Italian aircraft bombed Dhahran, where CASOC was headquartered" (ARAMCO).

1950

Saudi Arabia adjusts its share of revenue to a fifty-fifty split, meaning that the oil companies get half and the government gets half. This tax significantly increased the government's revenue, and soon after, other countries like Kuwait and Qatar followed suit.

1951-1960

Due to the massive increase in government revenue, the Saudi Arabia economy grew and much infrastructure was built, including more roads, airports, and hospitals.

Construction of Trans-Arabian Pipeline, a oil pipe from Qaisumah in Saudi Arabia to Sidon in Lebanon, was finished in 1950 and began use. The pipe increased the global trade of petroleum, increased America and Saudi Arabia's relationship, and helped grow Lebanon economically.

1961

On June 19, 1961, Kuwait gained independence from Britain "after being under their rule for 62 years" (Al Mulla). Kuwait therefore now had control over its own oil production.

1962-1970

Saudi Arabia's economy continued to grow further. Due to partnership with US construction companies, the government was modernized, television and broadcast facilities were built, and its defense industry expanded (Wright 152).

The Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries ("OPEC"), founded in 1960, now began restructuring the global system for oil in the favor of its member countries. They fought back against the Seven Sisters oil firms, which were owned by America, British, and Dutch companies, aiming to restore national sovereignty over natural resources.

1971

Bahrain, Qatar, and UAE became independent from British rule all in 1971 (Urkevich). This drew more power away from foreign actors on the oil industry.

1973

Due to the Yom Kippur War, the Organization of Arab Petroleum Exporting Countries ("OAPEC"), which consists of countries like Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Syria, and Kuwait, implemented an oil embargo against countries that supported Israel in the war. As a result, oil prices immediately shot up and over doubled in price (US Government). This event became known as the 1973 oil crisis.

Even though they ended the embargo in 1974, oil prices continued to rise, and the event made other countries realize they maybe shouldn't be so reliant on OAPEC oil. But as a natural side effect, the increase in oil prices generated OAPEC countries massive amounts of money, and for countries like Saudi Arabia, also more politcal influence.

1974-1976

Saudi Arabia became the largest oil producer in the world. Infrastructure and education continued to be developed and advanced, and they became closer tied to the United States. By this point, the goverment had 60% ownership of Aramco.

UAE also founded the Abu Dhabi Investment Authority ("ADIA"), which invests money from budget surpluses (mostly money from oil revenue) into stocks, bonds, real estate, etc. The ADIA has generated UAE massive amounts of money, having about $1.1 trillion in assets today (SWFI).

1980-1989

In Saudi Aarbia, more technological advancements came: the city became more urbanized and skyscrapers were built, public education became massively available, and more media outlets were made. Also this year, they claimed 100% full ownership of Aramco, generating the government massive amounts of money.

The 80s saw an oversupply in the amount of oil harvested versus the demand, and as a result, oil prices had crashed to below $10 per barrel in 1986 versus $35 per barrel in 1980. This event became known as the 1980s oil glut, and it was partially due to countries learning to use less oil as a result of the 1973 crisis.

1990-1991

In 1990, under Saddam Hussein's rule, Iraq invaded Kuwait; this started the Gulf War. Iraq forces burned hundreds of Kuwait's oil wells, and as a result, Kuwait's oil production stopped almost completely, leading to oil prices to nearly double in the few months after the onset of the war. Similar to the 1973 oil crisis, this event again showed the world how vulnerable and volatile the oil market is.

1997-1999

In 1997, the Asian financial crisis, a major recession in the Asia region, occured and drastically reduced the area's need for oil. Oil prices dipped to nearly the lowest they've ever been since the discovery of oil the Saudi Arabia, reaching about $11 per barrel.

2001-2007

The 9/11 attacks resulted in a short-term steep increase in oil prices, though prices stabilized in the following months. Afterwards, US went to invasion with Afghanistan, which didn't directly impact oil production, but rose global tensions and demand for oil, contributing to higher oil prices slowly over time.

2008-2009

The 2008 global financial crisis lessened the demand for oil and generated economic uncertainty, which resulted in an instant decrease in oil prices. Even to this day, oil prices still have not gone as high as they were before the financial crisis.

2010-2014

In 2010, the self-immolation of Mohamed Bouazizi occured, which we've talked about in this class before, and it resulted in a wave of Arab protests across the region, some of which are still going on to this day. Libya's government was overthrown and foreign oil operators were forced to leave, resulting in a 75% decrease in oil production (Jaffe).

At the same time, the US shale oil technologies improved, thus making the US less dependent on Gulf oil and increasing global oil supply.

2016-2019

In 2016, Saudi Arabia's goverment launched Saudi Vision 2030, a long-term govermnet initative desgined to diversify the country's economy, society, and culturue by reducing its economic dependence on oil; they plan to increase urbanization and international tourism. The initiative has been critisicized due to human right violations such as poor working conditions that have resulted in over 21,000 worker deaths on the job and displacement of hundreds of thousands of people due to mega projects like Neom.

2020-Present

In 2020, the COVID-19 pandemic's lockdowns and reduced travel resulted in steep decrease in demand of oil. Gulf countries faced major revenue losses, justifying some of their projects like Saudi Vision 2030.

In the present day, the oil market continues to be volatile and uncertain. On Febuary 28th 2026, Donald Trump declared war on Iran, causing oil prices to jump nearly 50% since the onset of the war. Gas pumps across the world have seen much higher prices.
